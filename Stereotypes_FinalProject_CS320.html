Introduction¶

Despite the fact that gender differences in educational outcomes has been narrowed impressively, boys have been showing better performance than girls in math. Math performance has been considered a good predictor of outcomes in the labor market. Recent studies have focused on explaining the gender gap in math performance in the context of gender stereotypes. If one has the prepreceptions that one gender generally performs worse in math than the other, it may cause discrimination. Stereotypes such as girls cannot perform as well as boys do in math could contribute to the grils' low confidence in math, and in turns, girls' worse performance in math. If stereotypes are communated by teachers, the effects can be more significant as their close interaction with students. Psychological studies have documented that teachers tend to believe math is more difficult for girls than boys. Girls exposed to such stereotypes communicated by their teachers are likley to be negatively affected in terms of their academic performance in math.

The main question of interest of this project is whether the exposure of students to teacher stereotypes has an impact on student academic performance in math. In addition, this projects attempts to predict teacher stereotypes given the characteristics of teachers.

Description of Data¶

The dataset used in this analysis is a survey data of 145 middle schools in Italy. The questionnare was anwered by around 1400 math and literature teachers. An implicit gender stereotypes value is calculated based on the teachers' response to the survey questions. The dataset also includes the information about students' background, standardized test score and teachers' backgroud (Michela Carlana, Implicit Stereotypes: Evidence from Teachers Gender Bias, The Quarterly Journal of Economics, Volume 134, Issue 3, August 2019, Pages 11631224).

Suppose we give some credibility to the story mentioned above, we can examine whether there are different relationships between math teacher stereotype and students' math scores for female and male students.

Part II. Establishing a Causal Link - Exogeneity of Assignment of Students to Teachers with Gender Stereotypes¶

Though we have inferred from regression results from Part I that math teachers' stereotypes have negative impact on female students' math performance, we cannot claim the relationship is causal. If students assigned to teachers with gender stereotypes are intrinsically different from the students assigned to teachers without gender stereotypes, the estimates of the impact of teachers' gender stereotypes on students' performance will be biased. Therefore, in this session, I run a few robustness checks to validate the exogeneity of assignment of students to teachers with gender stereotypes. Simply speaking, we want to see a statistically insignificant relationship between the related students characteristics with teachers' stereotype, which will supports that students exposed to teachers' gender stereotypes are have no baseline difference than the students not exposed to teachers' gender stereotypes.

Conclusion¶

In this project, the main question I investigate is whether math teachers' gender stereotypes have an impact on students' math performance. And I also conduct a robustness check for the exogeneity of the assignment of students to teachers with gender stereotypes. In summary, teachers' gender stereotypes have negative impacts on female students' math performace. And there is no supporting evidence that students assigned to teachers with different level of gender stereotypes are different, therefore validate the causal relationship between teachers' gender stereotypes and the worse math performance of female students.

In addition, the last part of this project focuses on the prediction of teachers' gender stereotype. With the regression tree model, one can predict the value of teachers' gender stereotype with known teacher baseline characteristics. With the KMeans Clustering Model, one can predict whether a teacher is with high gender stereotypes or low gender stereotypes. One should note that thess models do not claim any causal relationship between teachers' attributes and the gender stereotypes. Any causal interpretation of the model should be careful.
